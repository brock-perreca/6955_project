\begin{abstract}
Understanding human walking at the level of joint angles, ground
reaction forces, and inter-limb timing matters for clinical
biomechanics, rehabilitation, and assistive device design.
Reinforcement-learning agents that imitate recorded human motion
offer a tempting cheaper substitute for instrumented motion-capture
labs: train a simulated body to track a reference, and the internal
forces and gait-cycle structure should fall out of the physics for
free. We ask whether that promise actually holds for a common
research configuration. Can phase-conditioned PPO with a
DeepMimic-style engineered reward, applied to MuJoCo Walker2d-v4
conditioned on real human inverse-kinematics reference data, recover
a gait that holds up against held-out force-plate ground truth from
the same subject? We train phase-aware PPO with a behavioral-cloning
warm-start and reference-state initialization, alongside a parallel
attempt at Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP). The phase-aware policies
visually walk, but a held-out biomechanical-realism scorecard against
the same subject's measured kinematics and ground reaction forces
tells a different story. None of our trained variants pass. Peak
vertical ground reaction force lands at 3.7 to 4.8 times body weight
against a reference of 1.1, and double-support fraction sits below
2\% against a reference of 23\%. Those are the kinetic signatures
of bouncing or running, not walking. The negative result motivates
two paths forward we did not get to: AMP at GPU-parallel scale,
where the discriminator escapes its small-data memorization regime,
or a return to muscle-actuated models, where physiological force
limits would make the running-gait failure mode structurally
unreachable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Understanding human walking at the level of muscle forces, joint
contact loads, and ground reaction forces matters for clinical
biomechanics, rehabilitation, and assistive device design. The
usual instrumented laboratory --- optical mocap, force plates,
EMG --- costs upwards of \$150{,}000. Reinforcement-learning
agents that imitate recorded human motion are a tempting
alternative: if a simulated body tracks a reference closely enough,
internal forces and gait-cycle structure should emerge from the
physics for free.

\paragraph{From the original proposal to a scaled-down question.}
This project began as a study of \emph{lab-to-field transfer in
musculoskeletal reinforcement learning}: 3D, 80-muscle MyoLeg
agents trained on lab-grade marker/force-plate/EMG references
versus markerless smartphone mocap from
OpenCap~\cite{uhlrich2023opencap}. That scope was infeasible for
one semester. We carried the spirit of the question into a
smaller setting: the 2D MuJoCo Walker2d-v4 planar
biped~\cite{todorov2012mujoco} with a single-subject IK reference
at 1.25 m/s.

\paragraph{This paper.}
We train phase-conditioned PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo,raffin2021sb3}
with a multi-term DeepMimic~\cite{peng2018deepmimic} reward and a
behavioral-cloning warm-start, then evaluate the visually walking
policies against held-out biomechanical ground truth from the
same subject's force-plate and IK data. We additionally implement
Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP)~\cite{peng2021amp,escontrela2022amp}
as a learned-reward comparison. The visually walking policies
produce running-gait kinetics; AMP at small environment counts
collapses to a near-zero style reward, consistent with the
GPU-parallel scaling the original paper required.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{DeepMimic~\cite{peng2018deepmimic}.}
The modern recipe for physics-based motion imitation: a
phase-indexed reference drives a dense reward of weighted
exponential penalties on pose, velocity, end-effector, and root
tracking error, with Reference State Initialization for uniform
phase coverage. Our reward and RSI follow this framework on a
planar biped; unlike DeepMimic, we evaluate against held-out
kinetic ground truth at test time.

\paragraph{Adversarial Motion Priors~\cite{peng2021amp,escontrela2022amp}.}
AMP replaces hand-crafted imitation terms with a learned LSGAN
discriminator over reference vs policy state-transitions plus a
small task reward. Escontrela et al.~\cite{escontrela2022amp}
trained at 4096 parallel environments on a GPU cluster; we reuse
their design at 8--16 CPU environments and reproduce the
small-scale collapse implicit in their parallelism requirement.
GAIL and AIRL~\cite{ho2016gail,fu2018airl} are the
discriminator-as-reward predecessors and share the same collapse
pathology; we implement AIRL alongside AMP for comparison.

\paragraph{Massively parallel deep RL~\cite{rudin2022parallel}.}
Rudin et al. cut legged-locomotion training time from hours to
minutes with thousands of GPU-parallel environments --- the
empirical anchor behind the AMP scaling requirement and our
planned MuJoCo MJX~\cite{mujocomjx} port.

\paragraph{Muscle-driven imitation~\cite{simos2025kinesis,cotton2025kintwin}.}
KINESIS and KinTwin are recent imitation pipelines on Hill-type
muscle humanoids: emergent activations correlate with recorded
EMG without explicit EMG terms, and the approach reproduces
able-bodied and impaired gait from markerless input. They
motivate the muscle-actuation direction in Sec.~\ref{sec:future},
where physiological force limits make our Walker2d failure mode
structurally unreachable.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

We formalize gait imitation as a Markov Decision Process
$M = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, T, r, \gamma)$ augmented with a
reference motion. Let $q$, $\dot q$ be Walker2d's joint positions
and velocities, $s_\text{base} = [q,\,\dot q]$. The reference is
a periodic sequence
$R = \{q^\text{ref}_t\}_{t=0}^{T-1}$ of bilateral hip, knee, and
ankle angles at 1.25 m/s, sampled at the simulator control rate.
A scalar phase $\varphi \in \{0,\dots,T-1\}$ advances by one per
step modulo $T$; the phase-conditioned observation is
\begin{equation}
  s = \big[\,s_\text{base}\;|\;q^\text{ref}_\varphi\;|\;\sin(2\pi\varphi/T)\;|\;\cos(2\pi\varphi/T)\,\big].
  \label{eq:obs}
\end{equation}
The agent maximizes
$\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}\big[\sum_t \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t, \varphi_t)\big]$
where $r$ encodes fidelity to $R$.

\paragraph{Held-out biomechanical evaluation.}
Separately, let $B^*$ be biomechanical metrics from the same
subject's force-plate and IK data: stride period, cadence,
double-support fraction, peak vertical ground reaction force (vGRF),
and per-joint range of motion. The realism gap is the deviation
$|B(\pi) - B^*|$, aggregated as a 0--4 progress score with a
$\pm 20\%$ credible band per metric
(Sec.~\ref{sec:scorecard-method}). The training reward sees only
$R$; the kinetic targets in $B^*$ are held out.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Phase-conditioned PPO + DeepMimic reward}
\label{sec:method-ppo}

The Walker2d-v4 default observation is phase-blind. The policy sees
joint angles and velocities but no information about where it is in
the gait cycle. We augment the observation with the reference pose
at the current phase plus $\sin\varphi$ and $\cos\varphi$ as in
Eq.~\ref{eq:obs}, and at the start of each episode we set the joint
angles, joint velocities, and forward velocity of the body to the
reference values for a uniformly sampled starting phase. This is
DeepMimic's Reference State Initialization. The forward-velocity
init matters: without it the body is dropped mid-stride at zero
forward speed and immediately falls.

The reward sums four DeepMimic tracking terms (exponential
penalties on joint pose, joint velocity, foot end-effector
position, and root height) and terminates the episode if forward
velocity drops below 0.3 m/s. Our best policy uses a worst-joint
pose aggregator (minimum over per-joint penalties) so the policy
cannot hide a stiff joint behind compliant ones.

\paragraph{Behavioral cloning warm-start.}
We pretrain on PD-controller rollouts inside the simulator before
PPO fine-tuning. Inverse-dynamics torques would ignore ground
reaction forces and be systematically wrong during stance; PD
rollouts inside the full simulator produce torques consistent
with stance-phase loading.

\subsection{Adversarial Motion Priors and AIRL}
\label{sec:method-amp}

As a comparison track we implement AMP~\cite{peng2021amp,escontrela2022amp}
on top of the same Walker2d environment. A discriminator is trained
on state-transition pairs in 12-dimensional joint space using the
LSGAN loss with a zero-centered gradient penalty on expert samples.
The bounded style reward $r_s = \max(0,\,1 - 0.25(D - 1)^2)$ replaces
the hand-crafted pose reward, combined with a forward-velocity task
reward as $r = w_g\,r_\text{task} + w_s\,r_s$ with $w_g = 0.35$,
$w_s = 0.65$. We additionally implement AIRL~\cite{fu2018airl} with
the same discriminator backbone and a state-only reward
parameterization.

\subsection{Held-out biomechanical-realism scorecard}
\label{sec:scorecard-method}

For each policy we run 6 deterministic rollouts of 2500 steps,
detect heel-strikes from per-foot contact-force rising edges with
a 0.5\,s debounce, and slice into per-stride windows. Per-stride
medians are computed for stride period, cadence, double-support
fraction, peak vGRF (body-weight units), per-joint ROM, and L-R
asymmetry. Reference values use the same methodology on Subject
1's force-plate and IK data. Each metric contributes to a 0--4
progress score via a $\pm 20\%$ credible band, scaling linearly
from 1.0 inside the band to 0 at its edge.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:experiments}

\paragraph{Hypotheses.}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
  \item[\textbf{H1.}] A phase-conditioned observation with multi-term
        imitation reward produces \emph{qualitatively} walking
        policies (balance maintained, bilateral foot alternation,
        forward translation) where a phase-blind policy with only
        forward velocity does not.
  \item[\textbf{H2.}] The multi-term reward is exploitable. Each
        unconstrained degree of freedom yields a characteristic
        Goodhart-style failure (ankle paddling, one-legged hopping,
        toe-walking, controlled forward fall, stand-and-wiggle).
  \item[\textbf{H3.}] AMP at fewer than $\sim$100 parallel environments
        produces discriminator collapse: style reward converges to
        near-zero, eliminating the style gradient.
  \item[\textbf{H4.}] (\emph{Headline.}) Even when H1 holds and the
        policy visually walks, no engineered-reward variant we tested
        passes the held-out biomechanical-realism scorecard against
        force-plate-derived targets.
  \item[\textbf{H5.}] Failure modes that look reward-driven can have
        structural causes such as joint-range limits in the
        simulator's MJCF. Diagnosing reward issues without first
        checking the kinematic feasibility of the reference is
        unsafe.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Domain and data.}
Simulator: MuJoCo 3.3.0~\cite{todorov2012mujoco} via Gymnasium
Walker2d-v4. Reference motion: inverse-kinematics reconstruction of
treadmill walking from the SimTK Coordination Retraining
dataset~\cite{simtkulrich} (Subject 1, baseline trial, 1.25 m/s),
processed into one clean gait cycle of 56 frames at 50 Hz and
resampled to 140 frames at 125 Hz via cubic spline. Policy network:
256$\times$256 MLP, tanh activations
(Stable-Baselines3~\cite{raffin2021sb3} defaults). AMP discriminator:
two hidden layers of 64 units, ELU. Training: 8 parallel CPU
environments, 5M steps per run, PPO with $n_\text{steps} = 2048$,
batch size 64, $\gamma = 0.99$, $\lambda_\text{GAE} = 0.95$, learning
rate $3\times10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Trained variants.}
Four post-pivot policies are evaluated against the scorecard:
\emph{Baseline (stock joints)};
\emph{Permissive hips} ($-30^\circ$ to $+60^\circ$ thigh range);
\emph{Constrained hips} ($-150^\circ$ to $+35^\circ$, $5^\circ$
of headroom over the reference's flexion peak); and
\emph{Best engineered (lead)}, the permissive MJCF plus the
worst-joint pose aggregator. AMP/AIRL collapse before producing
clean strides and are evaluated on training-time
discriminator/style-reward curves (Sec.~\ref{sec:amp}).

\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Phase-conditioned PPO produces visually walking policies (H1)}
\label{sec:results-h1}

All four variants converge to forward-translating policies within
5M steps (Fig.~\ref{fig:ppo-curves}), with episode lengths reaching
3500--5500 steps and returns 1100--3800. Visual inspection of
deterministic rollouts confirms balance, foot alternation, and
forward progression near target speed. H1 is supported. Notably,
the variant that ranks highest on the held-out scorecard ranks
\emph{lowest} on training return (\emph{Best engineered}, blue):
the worst-joint aggregator is harder to optimize but closes a
reward-hacking loophole. Training-time return is not a reliable
proxy for biomechanical realism.

\subsection{Reward exploits are structured Goodhart cases (H2)}
\label{sec:exploits}

The multi-term reward arrived through iterative refinement. At each
stage, the optimizer found a locally optimal degenerate strategy,
and a new term was added to close it. Table~\ref{tab:exploits}
catalogues the characteristic exploits, each locally optimal for
the partial reward as written. None required pathological optimizer
behavior. H2 is supported.

  \label{tab:exploits}

    \toprule
    \textbf{Exploit} & \textbf{Mechanism} & \textbf{Closing patch} \\
    \midrule
    Ankle paddling & Reference ankle oscillates regardless of foot

    reward with no forward progress. & Direct swing-foot contact
    penalty $-w_{\text{swing}}\tanh(F_{\text{swing}}/50)$. \\
    \addlinespace[2pt]
    One-legged hopping & Planting one foot and using the other as a

    thigh range, the hip parks at the $+0^\circ$ wall (reference

\subsection{AMP collapses at small environment count (H3)}
\label{sec:amp}

We trained AMP and AIRL (including warm-started variants from a
working PPO policy) on the same environment. At 8 and 16 parallel
environments, the style reward $r_s$ never enters the target band
$[0.5, 1.0]$: it starts at 0.22--0.25 and decays toward the
collapse floor $\sim$0.03 over 5M steps
(Fig.~\ref{fig:amp-collapse}), with discriminator loss plummeting
as separation becomes near-perfect. The mechanism is structural:
$\sim$140 expert transitions on a 1-D manifold embedded in a 24-D
input vs only 8--16 environments of policy diversity --- the
discriminator has far more capacity than data and memorizes. The
16-env variant collapses more slowly (final $r_s = 0.06$) but
does not escape. Escontrela et al.~\cite{escontrela2022amp} ran
4096 environments on a GPU cluster; the path out is GPU-parallel
training (Sec.~\ref{sec:future}). H3 is supported.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../docs/figures/amp_collapse_paper.png}
  \caption{AMP discriminator collapse at small environment count.
  Style reward (left) never enters the target band (green); 8-env
  warm-started variants converge to the collapse floor of 0.03,
  16-env variants to 0.06. Discriminator loss (right, log scale)
  drops as the discriminator achieves near-perfect separation,

\subsection{No engineered-reward variant passes the biomechanical-realism scorecard (H4)}
\label{sec:scorecard}

This is the central result of the paper. Table~\ref{tab:scorecard}
shows per-metric values for the four variants against the measured
Subject 1 baseline. Every variant misses every spatiotemporal and
kinetic target by $\geq 40\%$. Stride period 0.57--0.67~s vs
1.12~s (cadence 1.7--2.0$\times$ too fast); peak vGRF
3.7--4.8$\times$ body weight vs 1.10; double-support 0.7--7\% vs
23\%. These are the kinetic signatures of running or bouncing,
not walking --- walking has $\sim$23\% double-support and peak
vGRF near body weight, running has near-zero double-support and
$\sim 2.5\times$ vGRF. Our values exceed even running.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The torque actuator has no mechanism by which producing 4 to 5
times body-weight ground reaction forces is more expensive than
producing $1\times$ — both are just torque commands. Closing the
gap inside this paradigm would require explicit reward terms on
double-support, vGRF magnitude, and stride period, which is no
longer imitation but hand-engineered gait shaping with the
imitation cost as a regularizer. We read the negative result as
structural to the engineered-reward + torque-actuated combination,
not as a tuning failure.

\subsection{Morphology can mask reward problems (H5)}
\label{sec:morph}

Earlier batches chased a ``stiff-hip'' failure (trained hip range
$\sim 2^\circ$ vs a reference asking for $\sim 45^\circ$). The
root cause was not the reward: stock \texttt{walker2d.xml}
constrains the thigh to $[-150^\circ, 0^\circ]$, while the
reference's flexion peak is $+30^\circ$. About 68\% of every
reference cycle lay outside the joint range, and the policy was
pinned at the $0^\circ$ wall for 95\% of frames
(Fig.~\ref{fig:hip-trace}). Opening the range produced an
order-of-magnitude jump in hip ROM. Methodological lesson: check
kinematic feasibility before tuning the reward.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Can engineered-reward phase-conditioned PPO on torque-actuated
Walker2d recover a biomechanically realistic gait against
held-out force-plate ground truth? The answer is \textbf{no}. The
visually walking policies produce running-gait kinetics (peak
vGRF 3.7--4.8 BW, near-zero double-support); AMP at 8--16
environments collapses before producing clean strides. The
engineered reward closes individual reward-hacking loopholes
through hand-engineering, but those loopholes do not include peak
vGRF or double-support, and no variant crosses the $\pm 20\%$
credible band on those metrics. The torque actuator does not
penalize 4-BW foot-slamming, and a reward shape that does
penalize it amounts to engineering walking by hand.

\paragraph{Two paths forward.}
First, port the environment to MuJoCo
MJX~\cite{mujocomjx} and train AMP at 2{,}000--4{,}000 parallel
environments on a single GPU, matching Escontrela et
al.~\cite{escontrela2022amp}. At that scale, policy diversity
forces the discriminator to generalize, and $r_s$ should escape
the collapse floor. Second, return to the original proposal's
muscle-actuated humanoid scope: KINESIS~\cite{simos2025kinesis}
and KinTwin~\cite{cotton2025kintwin} demonstrate that
muscle-driven imitation reproduces able-bodied and impaired gait,
including from markerless mocap. Hill-type muscles cannot produce
$4\times$ BW peak vGRF without violating force-length-velocity
limits --- the muscle actuator is, in effect, the implicit
biomechanical-realism prior we tried to construct as a reward.
Either path, evaluated against the same scorecard, would directly
measure the realism gap this paper documents.
